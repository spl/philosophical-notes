What is

the principle of charity?

Wilson

N.L. Wilson coined1 the term Principle of Charity.

1 Wilson, N L. Substances without Substrata. The Review of Metaphysics, vol. 12, no. 4, Philosophy Education Society Inc., 1959, pp. 521–39.

It referred to making the largest possible number of statements true.

Wilson

Charles says:

  1. Caesar conquered Gaul.
  2. Caesar crossed the Rubicon.
  3. Caesar was murdered on the Ides of March.
  4. Caesar was addicted to the use of the ablative absolute.
  5. Caesar was married to Boudica.

But Caesar does not refer to the same person in each statement.

Wilson

We select as designatum [of a name] that individual which will make the largest possible number of [the speaker’s] statements true.
  1. Caesar conquered Gaul.
  2. Caesar crossed the Rubicon.
  3. Caesar was murdered on the Ides of March.
  4. Caesar was addicted to the use of the ablative absolute.
  5. Caesar was married to Boudica.
  6. (Prasutagus was married to Boudica.)

Quine

W.V.O. Quine wrote2 about the indeterminacy of reference involved with radical translation.

2 Quine, W V O. Word and Object. Cambridge, Mass: Massachusetts Institute of Technology, 1960.

Quine

Suppose:

  1. You’re a linguist. 👄
  2. You’re standing next to CowDuck. 👥
  3. CowDuck speaks a radically different language. 🗣
  4. You’re building a model of that language. 📜
    5. (And you use TerminusDB to store that model. 💻)
  5. A rabbit scurries by. 🐇
  6. CowDuck says, Gavagai! 😯

What do you, as a linguist, think? 🤔

Quine

Of course, gavagai means rabbit! Right?

Quine: not necessarily. It might refer to:

  • a rabbit-fly, unknown to you, but recognizable some way off by its long wings and erratic movements
  • undetached rabbit-stuff
  • a phrase of superstition or tradition uttered when a rabbit appears

You sneeze. Bless you!

Quine

The maxim of translation underlying all this is that assertions startlingly false on the face of them are likely to turn on hidden differences of language.

Quine

The common sense behind the maxim is that one’s interlocutor’s silliness, beyond a certain point, is less likely than bad translation — or, in the domestic case, linguistic divergence.

Davidson

Donald Davidson applied Quine’s principle of charity more broadly to:

  • minimize disagreement or
  • maximize agreement.

Davidson

The aim of interpretation is not agreement but understanding. My point has always been that understanding can be secured only by interpreting in a way that makes for the right sort of agreement.3

3 Davidson, Donald. Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1984.

Davidson

[T]he principle directs [one to interpret] so as to read some of [one’s] own standards of truth into the pattern of sentences held true by the speaker. The point of the principle is to make the speaker intelligible, since too great deviations from consistency and correctness leave no common ground on which to judge either conformity or difference.4

4 Davidson, Donald. Subjective, Intersubjective, Objective. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 2001.

Jackman

Henry Jackman revisited5 Wilson’s principle and generalized it.

5 Jackman, Henry. Charity, Self-Interpretation, and Belief. Journal of Philosophical Research, vol. 28. 2003, pp. 145-170.

The semantic values of the words in a speaker’s language are the values in the set that maximizes the truth of the speaker’s commitments.

Critical thinking

When interpreting an unclear passage, always give the speaker or writer the benefit of the doubt. Never attribute to an arguer a weaker argument when the evidence reasonably permits us to attribute to him or her a stronger one. And never interpret a passage as a bad argument when the evidence reasonably permits us to interpret it as not an argument at all.6

6 Bassham, Gregory, et al. Critical Thinking: A Student’s Introduction, 5th ed. New York: Mc Graw Hill, 2013.

Why should you care

about the principle of charity?

Charity in self-interpretation

Recall Jackman’s conception:

The semantic values of the words in a speaker’s language are the values in the set that maximizes the truth of the speaker’s commitments.
  • There may be no such value set that makes all of our commitments true: there may be conflicts.
  • But we cannot help but interpret ourselves such that our most deeply held commitments are true.
  • Otherwise, we re-evaluate our beliefs.

Charity in interpreting others

What is your primary goal in a dialogue?

  • Winning the argument
  • Being right
  • Agreeing on shared truths
  • Understanding their perspective

Aim for understanding someone

  • It requires discovering how they see the world we share with them.
  • A good interpretation allows us to see the world through their eyes.
  • Try to understand someone as they would, ideally, understand themselves.
  • The principle of charity follows directly from that.

Why choose charity?

  • It shows respect.
  • It keeps the dialogue on a rational track.
  • It presents you with opportunities to persuade.
  • Constant practice develops your judgment.
  • It is the decent thing to do.
