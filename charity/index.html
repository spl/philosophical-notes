Sean Leather

What is the principle of charity?

Wilson

N.L. Wilson coined1 the term “Principle of Charity.”

1 Wilson, N L. “Substances without Substrata.” The Review of Metaphysics, vol. 12, no. 4, Philosophy Education Society Inc., 1959, pp. 521–39.

It referred to making the largest possible number of statements true.

Wilson

Charles says:

  1. Caesar conquered Gaul.
  2. Caesar crossed the Rubicon.
  3. Caesar was murdered on the Ides of March.
  4. Caesar was addicted to the use of the ablative absolute.
  5. Caesar was married to Boudica.

But “Caesar” does not refer to the same person in each.

Wilson

We select as designatum [of a name] that individual which will make the largest possible number of [the speaker’s] statements true.

Quine

W.V.O. Quine wrote2 about the indeterminacy of reference involved with radical translation.

2 Quine, W V O. Word and Object. Cambridge, Mass: Massachusetts Institute of Technology, 1960.

Quine

Suppose:

  1. You're a linguist. 👄
  2. You're standing next to Termy. 👥
  3. Termy speaks a radically different language. 🗣
  4. You're building a model of that language. 📜
  5. You use TerminusDB to store that model. 💻
  6. A rabbit scurries by. 🐇
  7. Termy says, “Gavagai!” 😯

What do you, as a Terminator linguist, think?

Quine

Of course, “gavagai” means rabbit! Right?

Quine: not necessarily. It might refer to:

  • a rabbit-fly, unknown to you, but recognizable some way off by its long wings and erratic movements
  • undetached rabbit-stuff
  • a phrase of superstition or tradition said when a rabbit appears

You sneeze. “Bless you!”

Quine

The maxim of translation underlying all this is that assertions startlingly false on the face of them are likely to turn on hidden differences of language.

Quine

The common sense behind the maxim is that one’s interlocutor’s silliness, beyond a certain point, is less likely than bad translation — or, in the domestic case, linguistic divergence.

Davidson

Donald Davidson applied Quine's principle of charity across the board to:

  • minimize disagreement or
  • maximize agreement.

Davidson

The aim of interpretation is not agreement but understanding. My point has always been that understanding can be secured only by interpreting in a way that makes for the right sort of agreement.3

3 Davidson, Donald. Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1984.

Davidson

[T]he principle directs [one to interpret] so as to read some of [one's] own standards of truth into the pattern of sentences held true by the speaker. The point of the principle is to make the speaker intelligible, since too great deviations from consistency and correctness leave no common ground on which to judge either conformity or difference.4

4 Davidson, Donald. Inquiries into Truth and Interpretation. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1984.
